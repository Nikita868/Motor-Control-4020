Module 1 · Lesson 2

Classifying Motor Skills

Four ways to read the demands of a task — building up to Gentile’s taxonomy, the tool you’ll use in Lab 1.

~30 min Reading: Schmidt & Lee, Ch. 1 Prepares Lab 1
Start here

Why classify a skill at all?

Before you can teach or rehabilitate a movement, you need to know what it demands of the learner — how much attention, memory, and decision-making it takes, and what physical control it requires. Classification is how you read those demands systematically instead of by gut feel.

Why it matters clinically

The same classification that describes a skill also tells you how to progress practice and how to chart a patient’s recovery. Classification isn’t labeling for its own sake — it’s the first step of program design.

We’ll build up through four systems. The first three are single sliders. The fourth — Gentile’s taxonomy — combines them into a full map, which is why it needs more than one look.

Continuous

Tap to reveal →
Arbitrary start and end; repetitive, runs until you decide to stop.
Steering, cycling, walking, rowing.
Watch the boundary

Serial skills are the tricky middle case — they’re discrete movements chained, so students often mislabel them as continuous. The test: can you point to distinct sub-actions with their own endpoints? If yes, it’s serial.

System 3 · The environment

Open vs. closed skills

This one carries the most weight for practice design, so it gets a closer look. The question is about the environmental context — what Gentile calls the regulatory conditions: the supporting surface, the objects involved, and other people or animals.

CLOSEDOPEN
Stationary environmentEnvironment in motion
The performer starts the action  |  the environment dictates timing

Closed skill

Tap to reveal →
Surface, objects, and people are stationary. The performer chooses when to begin.

Picking up a cup at a table · typing · a golf shot · walking an empty hallway.

Open skill

Tap to reveal →
Something in the environment is in motion, so the environment dictates timing.

Catching a thrown ball · returning a serve · driving on a freeway · a jump shot in traffic.
Why this one matters most

Open skills are typically harder: tracking moving objects costs attention, and timing must be decided under pressure. That’s the core progression principle you’ll use all semester — start closed, build toward open as a learner or patient gains capacity.

Now combine them

Gentile’s taxonomy doesn’t replace these systems — it crosses two of them into a single map of skill difficulty. Let’s build it one dimension at a time.

System 4 · Gentile’s taxonomy

Two dimensions, built up

Antoinette Gentile (2000) proposed reading every motor skill along two dimensions at once. Each dimension splits into two sub-questions, which is what turns a simple open/closed call into a 4×4 grid of sixteen categories.

1 Environmental context — “what is the world doing?”

This extends the open/closed idea you just learned, split into two sub-questions:

Regulatory conditionsStationary or in-motion? (This is the closed vs. open distinction.)
Inter-trial variabilityDo the conditions stay the same from one attempt to the next, or change each time?
Inter-trial variability, concretely

Walking through a room where objects sit in different places each time has variability — every pass needs different movements. A billiards shot always racked in the same spot has none. Same regulatory category, very different demand.

2 Function of the action — “what is the body doing?”

The second dimension describes the performer, again as two sub-questions:

Body orientationIs the body stationary (static) or does it change location (transport) during the skill?
Object manipulationIs the performer handling an object, or none at all?
The organizing principle

Read the grid from top-left to bottom-right: the simplest skills (stable world, still body, no object, no variability) sit top-left; demand climbs steadily to the most complex (moving world, moving body, object to manage, variability every trial) at bottom-right.

Why clinicians love it

The taxonomy as a progression tool

Because the grid is ordered by difficulty, it doubles as a roadmap for practice. You can locate where a patient currently succeeds, then step them one cell toward the bottom-right — adding motion, then an object, then variability — charting progress as a profile of competencies rather than a single pass/fail.
