Classifying Motor Skills

Four ways to read the demands of a task — building up to Gentile’s taxonomy, the tool you’ll use in Lab 1.

~30 min Schmidt & Lee, Ch. 1 Prepares Lab 1
Start here

Why classify a skill at all?

Before you can teach or rehabilitate a movement, you need to know what it demands of the learner.

Classification tells you the cognitive load (attention, memory, decision-making) and the physical control a task requires — systematically, rather than by gut feel.

Why it matters clinically

The same classification that describes a skill also tells you how to progress practice and how to chart a patient’s recovery. Classification isn’t labeling for its own sake — it’s the first step of program design.

You’ll meet four systems. The first three are single sliders. The fourth — Gentile’s taxonomy — combines them into a full map.

Discrete, serial, and continuous

Watch the boundary

Serial skills are the tricky middle case — discrete movements chained, so they’re often mislabeled continuous. The test: can you point to distinct sub-actions with their own endpoints? If yes, it’s serial.

System 3 · The environment

Open vs. closed skills

This one carries the most weight for practice design. The question is about the environmental context — what Gentile calls the regulatory conditions: the supporting surface, the objects involved, and other people or animals.

CLOSEDOPEN
Stationary environmentEnvironment in motion
The performer starts the action  |  the environment dictates timing
Why this one matters most

Open skills are typically harder: tracking moving objects costs attention, and timing is decided under pressure. That’s the progression principle you’ll use all semester — start closed, build toward open.

Now combine them

Gentile’s taxonomy doesn’t replace these systems — it crosses two of them into a single map of skill difficulty.

We’ll build it one dimension at a time.

Gentile’s taxonomy · Dimension 1 of 2

What is the world doing?

Antoinette Gentile (2000) read every motor skill along two dimensions. The first extends the open/closed idea you just learned — split into two sub-questions.

1 Environmental context
Regulatory conditionsStationary or in-motion? (This is closed vs. open.)
Inter-trial variabilityDo conditions stay the same from one attempt to the next, or change each time?
Inter-trial variability, concretely

Walking a room where objects sit in different places each time has variability — every pass needs different movements. A billiards shot always racked in the same spot has none. Same regulatory category, very different demand.

Gentile’s taxonomy · Dimension 2 of 2

What is the body doing?

The second dimension describes the performer — again as two sub-questions.

2 Function of the action
Body orientationIs the body stationary (static) or does it change location (transport) during the skill?
Object manipulationIs the performer handling an object, or none at all?
The organizing principle

Cross the two dimensions and you get sixteen categories. Read them top-left to bottom-right: simplest (stable world, still body, no object, no variability) climbing to most complex (moving world, moving body, object to manage, variability every trial).
